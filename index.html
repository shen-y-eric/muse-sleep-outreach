music and sleep
sleep deprivation is a pressing issue, especially among college students. chronic sleep deprivation can have devastating health consequences, including weakening your immune system, reducing the effectiveness of vaccines, and increasing the risk of obesity.

fortunately, music has been cited as a sleep aid that can help you feel relaxed and at ease. given musicâ€™s accessibility and potential sleep benefits, it might be a good time to try adding it to your nightly routine.
the research
a 2005 study found that in elders (60-83 years) who had trouble sleeping, listening to sedative music for 45 minutes before sleeping resulted in significantly better sleep quality. components of sleep quality included better perceived sleep quality, longer sleep duration, less sleep disruption, and less daytime dysfunction. importantly, there was a cumulative dose effect, which meant that sleep quality continued to improve every week.

a 2019 study found that not only did listening to music before naps improve subjective sleep quality, it also led to a decrease of Stage 1 sleep and increase in SWS sleep, which is important for memory consolidation.

another study found that in older women experiencing imsomnia, adding self-selected music to their sleep routines helped reduce the time to fall asleep from 27-69 minutes to 6-13 minutes.

a study that looked at college students with sleep complaints found that music statistically significantly improved sleep quality, and also statistically significantly reduced depressive symptoms
why music works
there hasn't been much research on the underlying mechanisms of music's effects of sleep, but music in general has been shown to reduce cortisol levels while also increasing oxytocin and dopamine levels. these can work together to ease stress and reduce chronic or acute pain, which are all common causes of sleep dysfunction.

music has also been shown to be a helpful distraction from frustration or anxious thoughts before bed, and it can also drown out environmental noises such as planes flying overhead, busy roads, dogs barking, or noisy neighbors.
